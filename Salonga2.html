Salonga Conservation Area

Salonga National Park, in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, has been designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1984. It is the largest rainforest reserve in Africa, covering 3.6 million hectares and encompassing parts of the Kasai, Mai-Ndombe, and Tshuapa provinces in the central part of the country.

Comprising two nearly pristine sections of humid tropical forest and plains, mainly on non-swampy terrain, the park protects populations of elephants and the curious bonobo (Pan paniscus).

The park was included in the List of World Heritage in Danger between 1984 and 1992 due to the decline in the white rhinoceros population, which was later reversed through the efforts of the World Heritage Committee, IUCN, WWF, the Frankfurt Zoological Society, and national authorities. Despite recent challenges posed by the civil war leading to attacks on the park's infrastructure, reports from various international conservation NGOs indicate that the park staff is effectively resisting poaching attempts.

A total of 56 fish species have been identified in the park, including the catfish species Clarias buthupogon and Synodontis nigriventris. Additionally, African slender-snouted crocodiles are also found within the park.
